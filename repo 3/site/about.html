The Editor

Aiden Goldenberg

I read government documents for a living, more or less. This is where I write down what's in them.

I'm a Juris Master candidate at Florida State University College of Law, studying Cybersecurity, Privacy, and Tech Risk. Before that I played Division I tennis at Florida State.

The two things are connected, but only once, and here it is: in tennis they keep a statistic for the points you lose entirely on your own. Not because your opponent did something brilliant — because you missed a shot you should have made. It's the most honest number on the sheet.

Then I started reading enforcement actions, and they read like the same statistic. A vendor sends a report. Nobody acts on it. A patch ships. Nobody installs it. Someone leaves the company and their administrator login stays switched on for three and a half years. There's rarely a genius on the other side of the net. There's usually a document, filed early, that nobody read.

That's the beat. That's the name. I'll stop talking about tennis now.

What this covers

Cybersecurity enforcement, privacy regulation, technology policy, and federal rulemaking — for anyone who wants to know what's actually happening rather than what a press release says is happening.

Two things run here. The Daily Docket goes up every morning: what the agencies filed, quoted from the filing, linked to the filing. No analysis, no summary, no adjectives. Reports come when there's something worth a longer look — usually a consent order or a rule that says more than anybody noticed.

I'm not a journalist by training and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. I'm someone who reads regulatory documents closely and thinks more people would get value out of them if somebody translated them without editorializing.

Why it's built the way it is

The tool that makes this possible at any scale is AI, and AI is very good at producing sentences that sound right and cite documents that don't say what they're claimed to say.

That isn't hypothetical. It happened on my first report. An AI wrote a sentence attributing a specific dollar figure to a legal order. The figure was real. The sentence was in the FTC's press release, not the order. Cited to the wrong document, stated with total confidence, and it would have been in print.

A plain text search caught it in under a second, because nothing gets published here until a search confirms the source sentence exists, word for word, in a document I've saved. That fact was deleted, and it's listed publicly with the six others that died with it.

The rest of how this place works is on the House Rules page. It's short.

Tell me I'm wrong

It's the thing I want most. If you think a fact here is wrong, tell me which one and why. If you're right, I'll correct it on the page, say what changed, and log how long it took me.
