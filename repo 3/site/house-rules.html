How this place works

House Rules

Five rules. They're short, they're enforced by software rather than willpower, and they explain most of what you'll notice about this site.

1. Only primary sources

Court filings. Agency orders. Published rules. Official advisories. Those are the only things we quote.

News coverage sometimes tells us where to look, and we're grateful for it. But it never appears as a source, and nothing we publish rests on it. The reason is simple: AI summarizers citing each other in a circle is a real and growing problem, and the only exit is to go read the document.

2. Facts before prose

Before a report is written, every fact it will state is listed out separately, each one pointing to the exact sentence in the source where it appears. The article gets assembled from the facts that survive.

This sounds fussy. It is the whole ballgame. Writing the article first and hunting for citations afterward is precisely the process that produces confident, fluent, fabricated references — and it's how AI-assisted reporting goes wrong.

3. Three checks, and anything that fails is deleted

A plain text search Does the quoted sentence actually appear in the document, word for word? No AI involved. It's a find-in-page. It costs nothing and it catches the invented citation, which is the failure that ends publications.
A second reader, shown only the quote An independent AI sees the quote and the claim — nothing else, not the article, not the framing. It answers one question: does this quote really support this claim? "Close enough" fails.
A third reader, told to prove us wrong A different AI, given the full document, is instructed to break each fact. Not to check it. To break it.

A fact that fails any of the three doesn't get softened or hedged. It gets deleted. If deleting it breaks the report, the report doesn't run.

4. No opinions, and it isn't up to the writer

"The breach was self-inflicted."   "The company was negligent."   "The attack was unsophisticated."

A filter scans every sentence before publication and rejects any that reads like a verdict. The editor cannot override it. It runs on his own biography page too.

Those are judgments. A court reaches them. We don't.

Here's the alternative, and we think it's better journalism anyway. The FTC's filing says a security vendor warned a company in January 2020. It says the intrusion began December 27, 2021. We print both dates. We stop. Twenty-three months is the finding, and you reached it yourself, which is the only place a conclusion belongs.

The name of this publication describes a pattern, not a person or a company. An unforced error is a point you lose on your own. We will never write that a specific company committed one.

5. We publish what we cut, and what we get wrong

Every report links to a page listing the facts that didn't survive, and why. The first report drafted 34 facts and cut 7 of them. One cited a dollar figure to a legal order; the search proved the sentence was from a press release instead. Another said a company "paid a ransom" when the document only says it "agreed to pay."

We don't hide those. They're the reason to believe the ones that made it.

The same applies to mistakes. If you find one, tell us. If you're right, we correct it on the page itself with a note saying what changed and when. Nothing is ever quietly edited.

What we don't claim

The checking system isn't perfect and we don't pretend otherwise. On the first report, the two AI checkers disagreed with each other — and the one we expected to be strictest was the one that let a bad sentence through. We published that finding alongside the report.

A verification system whose weaknesses you can't inspect isn't a verification system. It's a logo.

Not legal advice. Nothing here is legal advice, and the editor is not your lawyer. When we describe an accusation from a government filing, we say it's an accusation, because that's what the record supports.

No money changes hands. No ads, no sponsors, no vendors, nobody paying for coverage.
